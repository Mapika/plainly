# The Positive Standard — What Good Prose Is

The standard `plainly` measures *toward*. These are durable, checkable rules from the
canonical style authorities. Fixes should move text in these directions — not just away
from tells. Each rule notes the authority's own stated exceptions, so judicious use is
rewarded, not blanket-penalized.

## Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style*

- **Omit needless words.** "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no
  unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences."
- **Use the active voice** — usually more direct and vigorous than the passive.
- **Use definite, specific, concrete language.** "Prefer the specific to the general, the
  definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract."
- **Make definite assertions.** Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, noncommittal language.
- **Put statements in positive form.** Tell the reader what *is*, not only what is not.

Source: <https://faculty.washington.edu/heagerty/Courses/b572/public/StrunkWhite.pdf>

## Orwell, "Politics and the English Language"

The six rules (verbatim):
1. Never use a metaphor, simile or figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an
   everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

The four faults to detect: **dying metaphors** (worn-out figures of speech), **operators /
verbal false limbs** (padding that replaces a simple verb), **pretentious diction**
(inflation dressed as sophistication), **meaningless words** (abstractions with no agreed
sense). Diagnostic ideal: "Good prose is like a window-pane."

Source: <https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/>

## Williams, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*

- **Make the main characters the subjects** of your sentences.
- **Make their important actions the verbs.** Muddy prose buries actions inside abstract
  nouns (**nominalizations**) and characters inside prepositional phrases.
- **Exception (do not over-correct):** keep a nominalization when it refers back to a prior
  sentence, replaces an awkward "the fact that," names the object of a verb, or names a
  familiar concept.

Source: <https://nysba.org/thoughts-on-legal-writing-from-the-greatest-of-them-all-joseph-m-williams-part-i/>

## Zinsser, *On Writing Well*

- Four articles of faith: **clarity, simplicity, brevity, humanity.**
- "Writing improves in direct ratio to the number of things we can keep out of it that
  shouldn't be there."
- **Bracket every word not doing useful work, then cut.** Targets: redundant particles
  (*order **up***), adverbs duplicating the verb (*smile **happily***), adjectives stating a
  known fact (***tall** skyscraper*), weak qualifiers (*a bit, sort of, rather*).
- Long → short: *utilize→use, facilitate→ease, numerous→many, sufficient→enough,
  attempt→try, referred to as→called.*

Source: <http://higinbotham.lmc.gatech.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/359/2016/06/Zinsser.pdf>

## Pinker, *The Sense of Style*

- **Classic style:** the writer directs the reader's gaze to something concrete in the
  world, rather than stacking abstraction on abstraction.
- **The curse of knowledge** is the chief cause of bad prose: experts forget what it was
  like not to know.
- Habits at odds with good prose: **metadiscourse** ("In this section we will discuss…"),
  **signposting**, **hedging**, **zombie nouns** (verbs embalmed as `-tion/-ance/-ing`
  nouns), **unnecessary passives**.

Source: <https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2012/11/exorcising-the-curse-of-knowledge/>

## The bottom line — strong prose scores high on

1. **Concrete nouns and specific facts** (not abstraction stacks).
2. **Strong active verbs; characters as subjects** (not nominalizations / gratuitous passives).
3. **Concision** (no filler, hedges, metadiscourse, redundant modifiers).
4. **Sentence-length variation / rhythm** (not uniform cadence).
5. **Showing over telling** (concrete detail, not asserted significance).
6. **Plain over inflated diction** (short word over Latinate substitute).
7. **No stale or borrowed imagery** (no clichés / ready-made phrases).
8. **A distinct, committed point of view** (not hedged, averaged, consensus prose).

These eight are the orthogonal dimensions a fix should improve. They are exactly the
profile generic AI prose most often lacks.
