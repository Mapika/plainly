# The Silk Road: A World Made by Trade

The Silk Road was never a single road. It was a shifting web of overland and maritime routes connecting China to the Mediterranean across Central Asia, the Indian subcontinent, and the Persian Gulf — a network that carried goods, ideas, and people for more than a thousand years.

The name, coined by a German geographer in 1877, romanticizes what was largely a practical undertaking. Merchants rarely traveled the full distance themselves; goods passed through dozens of hands, each caravan moving one segment of the journey before trading on to the next. Silk was the premium commodity, valued in Rome and Byzantium for its weight in silver, but the real traffic was more varied: spices, glassware, horses, cotton, paper, and eventually gunpowder and printing.

What traveled alongside trade goods was often more consequential. Buddhism spread from India into Central Asia and China along Silk Road corridors. Islam followed trade routes into sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia. The Black Death, devastating as it was, reached Europe in part by the same networks. Mathematical concepts, musical instruments, and agricultural techniques crossed continents through the same channels that moved silk and pepper.

The routes declined as maritime trade expanded in the 15th and 16th centuries, offering faster and cheaper connections between East and West. But the Silk Road's legacy endures: it is the reason civilizations that never directly met nonetheless share common threads in their art, religion, and material culture.

Human connection, it turns out, finds a way.
