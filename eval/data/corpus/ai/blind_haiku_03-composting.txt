# Getting Started with Home Composting

Composting sounds complicated until you realize it's just controlled rot. Kitchen scraps and yard waste break down into rich soil. It takes minimal effort and transforms waste into something valuable for your garden.

Start simple. You need three things: a bin (a plastic container with drainage holes works fine), brown materials (dried leaves, shredded paper, cardboard), and green materials (vegetable scraps, grass clippings, coffee grounds). Alternate layers—think lasagna. The browns provide carbon and structure; the greens provide nitrogen and moisture.

Keep the pile as moist as a wrung-out sponge. Turn it every week or two with a shovel. Within a few months in warm weather, you'll have dark, crumbly compost that smells like forest floor. In cooler climates, expect three to six months.

Avoid meat, dairy, and oils—they attract pests and smell. But almost everything else from your kitchen is fair game. Eggshells, banana peels, vegetable scraps, tea leaves. Your compost becomes a small ecosystem, teeming with bacteria and fungi doing the work for you.

The payoff is tangible. Free fertilizer. Less trash in your bin. Knowing that what you grew fed you, and what was left over feeds your next season's garden. That's the quiet satisfaction of composting.
