# How to Start Composting at Home (Even If You Have No Idea Where to Begin)

Composting sounds like something that requires a large yard, a specific personality type, and a tolerance for mess. In reality, it requires a bin, some kitchen scraps, and a little patience. Here is how to start.

**Choose your bin.** For most beginners, a simple plastic tumbler or a lidded bin from a garden center works well. If you have outdoor space, an open pile in a corner of the yard is just as effective. No outdoor space? A small countertop bin feeding into a worm composter (vermicomposting) handles apartment life nicely.

**Know what goes in.** Compost thrives on balance: roughly half "greens" (nitrogen-rich materials like vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and fresh grass clippings) and half "browns" (carbon-rich materials like cardboard, dried leaves, and newspaper). Avoid meat, dairy, and oily foods — they attract pests and create odors.

**Maintain it.** Turn or tumble your compost every week or two to introduce oxygen, which speeds decomposition and prevents bad smells. If it smells sour, add more browns. If it looks too dry, add a splash of water or more greens.

In two to three months, you will have finished compost — dark, crumbly, and smelling faintly of earth. Work it into garden beds or potted plants, and watch things grow.

That is genuinely all there is to it.
