# The Silk Road: History's Great Connector

The Silk Road was never a single road, and it carried far more than silk. It was a sprawling web of caravan routes and sea lanes that, for some 1,500 years, stitched together the great civilizations of Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Europe. Stretching roughly from the imperial capitals of China to the bustling ports of the Mediterranean, it formed the closest thing the ancient world had to a worldwide web.

The network took shape around the second century BCE, when the Han dynasty pushed westward and opened secure passage across Central Asia. Chinese silk flowed toward Rome, where it became so coveted that senators fretted about gold draining out of the empire. Coming the other way were spices, glassware, horses, precious stones, and gold.

But the road's deepest cargo was intangible. Along these routes traveled Buddhism out of India, Islam across Central Asia, and ideas in mathematics, astronomy, paper-making, and gunpowder. Languages mingled, art forms blended, and entire cities like Samarkand grew rich as crossroads of the world.

The Silk Road also carried what no one wanted, including the diseases that may have helped spread the Black Death. Its lesson endures: connection brings prosperity and peril together, and the exchange of goods is never far from the exchange of ideas.
