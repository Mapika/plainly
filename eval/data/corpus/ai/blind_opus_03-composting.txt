# Composting at Home: A Beginner's Guide

Composting sounds like something that requires a backyard, a degree in soil science, and a tolerance for bad smells. In reality, it's just a way of letting nature do what it already does best: turn scraps into rich, dark earth. Here's how to start without overthinking it.

First, choose a container. A simple lidded bin in a corner of the yard works, and so does a small countertop pail if you live in an apartment. The size matters less than having somewhere consistent to collect your material.

The secret to good compost is balance. Think of it as feeding two ingredients: "greens" and "browns." Greens are wet and nitrogen-rich, like fruit peels, vegetable scraps, and coffee grounds. Browns are dry and carbon-rich, like dead leaves, shredded cardboard, and torn newspaper. Aim for roughly two parts brown to one part green. Skip meat, dairy, and oily foods, which attract pests and turn rank.

Every week or so, give the pile a turn with a fork or shovel. This adds air, which keeps things breaking down sweetly rather than smelling sour.

In a few months, the bottom of your pile becomes crumbly, earthy compost. Spread it on your garden or houseplants, and watch your waste become something that grows.
