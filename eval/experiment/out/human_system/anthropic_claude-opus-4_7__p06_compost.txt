# A Beginner's Guide to Composting at Home

Composting is just controlled rotting. That's the whole secret. Once you stop being precious about it, the rest is easy.

Start with a bin. A plastic tumbler from the hardware store works, but so does a wooden crate or a corner of the yard with a few pallets nailed together. If you're stuck in an apartment, look up bokashi or worm bins — both fit under a sink.

The trick is balance. You need "greens" (kitchen scraps, coffee grounds, grass clippings) for nitrogen and "browns" (dry leaves, shredded cardboard, torn-up egg cartons) for carbon. Aim for roughly two parts brown to one part green by volume. Too much green and your pile turns into a slimy, smelly mess. Too much brown and nothing happens at all.

Keep these out: meat, dairy, oily food, pet waste, and anything labeled compostable plastic — most of it doesn't actually break down at home.

Turn the pile every week or two with a pitchfork or shovel to add air. Sprinkle water if it feels drier than a wrung-out sponge.

In three to six months, you'll have dark, crumbly compost that smells like a forest floor. Spread it on your garden beds and start over.
