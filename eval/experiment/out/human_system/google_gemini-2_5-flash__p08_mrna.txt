mRNA vaccines teach our bodies to fight off viruses. Here's how: they deliver a genetic blueprint, called messenger RNA (mRNA), into our cells. This particular mRNA carries instructions for making a harmless piece of the virus, often a "spike protein" found on its surface.

Once inside our cells, the mRNA acts like a recipe. Our cellular machinery reads these instructions and starts producing many copies of that spike protein. It's important to remember that this protein alone cannot cause illness; it's just a fragment.

Our immune system then spots these foreign spike proteins. It recognizes them as invaders, even though they're harmless, and mounts a defense. This involves creating antibodies and specialized immune cells that remember how to neutralize the spike protein.

Later, if we encounter the actual virus, our immune system already knows what to do. It quickly recognizes the spike protein on the real virus and launches a swift, effective attack, preventing us from getting sick or reducing the severity of the illness. The mRNA itself is temporary; it degrades naturally within a few days, having done its job.
