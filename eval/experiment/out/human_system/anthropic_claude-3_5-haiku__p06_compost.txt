Composting: Your Kitchen Scraps' Second Life

Composting transforms kitchen and yard waste into rich, dark soil that'll make your garden thrive. Getting started is simpler than you might think.

First, choose a bin. You can buy a purpose-built composter, build a wooden structure, or even start with a large plastic container with holes drilled in the bottom. Place it in a sunny spot with good drainage.

What goes in? Green materials like fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and fresh grass clippings provide nitrogen. Brown materials — dry leaves, cardboard, wood chips — add carbon. Aim for a rough 3:1 ratio of brown to green.

Skip meat, dairy, and oily foods, which attract pests. Chop larger items into smaller pieces to speed decomposition. Keep the pile moist but not soaking — think of a wrung-out sponge.

Turn your compost every week or two with a pitchfork or shovel. This adds oxygen and helps break down materials faster. In a few months, you'll see dark, crumbly soil that smells like earth after rain.

Troubleshooting is part of the process. A smelly pile means too much moisture or not enough turning. No decomposition? Your mix might need more green materials or water.

Patience is key. Nature works slowly, but your garden will thank you.
