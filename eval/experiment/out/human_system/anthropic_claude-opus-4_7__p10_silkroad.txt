# The Silk Road

The Silk Road wasn't a road. It was a sprawl of caravan tracks, mountain passes, and sea routes that linked China to the Mediterranean for roughly fifteen centuries. The name itself is a nineteenth-century invention, coined by the German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen in 1877. The traders who actually walked these routes would not have recognized the term.

Things got moving under the Han dynasty around 130 BCE, when the emperor Wu sent the envoy Zhang Qian west looking for military allies. He came back with intelligence about kingdoms no one in China had heard of. Trade followed. Chinese silk reached Rome, where senators complained it was bankrupting the empire and corrupting Roman women with its near-transparency.

Silk gets the headlines, but the cargo was more interesting than that. Paper, gunpowder, and the stirrup moved west. Glass, horses, and grapes moved east. Buddhism made its way from India into China along these same tracks, carried by monks and merchants. So did the bubonic plague, which probably hitched a ride on Mongol caravans in the 1340s.

By the late 1400s, ocean shipping made overland routes obsolete, and the dust settled on the old caravan stops.
