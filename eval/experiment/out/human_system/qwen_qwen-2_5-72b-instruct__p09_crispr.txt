CRISPR gene editing is a revolutionary technology that allows scientists to make precise changes to DNA. The name stands for "Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats," referring to the bacterial defense system that inspired the technique. CRISPR uses a guide RNA to locate the specific DNA sequence to be edited. Once the target is found, an enzyme called Cas9 acts like molecular scissors, cutting the DNA at the precise location. This break triggers the cell's natural repair mechanisms, which can either disable a gene or insert new genetic material.

The process is straightforward in concept but sophisticated in execution. First, scientists design a guide RNA that matches the DNA sequence they want to edit. This guide RNA pairs with the Cas9 enzyme. When the guide RNA finds its target, Cas9 cuts the DNA. The cell then tries to repair the break, but this repair can introduce errors, effectively disabling the gene. Alternatively, scientists can provide a template DNA sequence that the cell uses to make precise edits, adding or replacing genetic information.

CRISPR's precision and ease of use have made it a powerful tool in genetics, with applications ranging from treating genetic diseases to improving crop resilience.
