The Silk Road was not a single road but a shifting web of land and sea routes that linked China, Central Asia, India, Persia, the Middle East, and the Mediterranean. It began to take shape during the Han dynasty, around the second century BCE, when Chinese envoys and traders pushed westward and found markets hungry for silk, jade, spices, horses, glassware, and precious metals.

Trade moved in stages. Few merchants crossed the whole distance from China to Rome. Instead, goods passed through oasis towns such as Kashgar, Samarkand, and Bukhara, where traders, translators, moneylenders, and guards made long-distance exchange possible. These cities became wealthy and cosmopolitan, shaped by Persian, Turkic, Chinese, Indian, Arab, and later Mongol influences.

The Silk Road mattered because it carried far more than luxury goods. Buddhism spread from India into Central Asia and China along these routes. Islam later moved eastward through merchants and scholars. Technologies such as papermaking traveled west, while crops, artistic styles, medical knowledge, and scientific ideas crossed in many directions.

The routes were also dangerous. War, bandits, deserts, mountain passes, and disease made travel costly. The Black Death likely moved along these networks in the fourteenth century. Even so, the Silk Road left a lasting mark: it made Eurasia more connected long before modern globalization.
