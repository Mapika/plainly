Composting turns kitchen scraps and yard waste into dark, crumbly soil food. You do not need a big garden to start. A small bin on a balcony or a lidded bucket under the sink can work.

First, choose a container. Outdoors, use a compost bin, tumbler, or a simple pile in a dry, shady spot. Indoors, pick a sealed compost bucket or a worm bin if you are comfortable with red wigglers.

Add two kinds of material: “greens” and “browns.” Greens include fruit peels, vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and tea leaves. Browns include dry leaves, shredded cardboard, paper egg cartons, and small twigs. Aim for more browns than greens. This helps prevent smells.

Do not add meat, dairy, oily food, pet waste, or diseased plants. These can attract pests or cause bad odors.

Keep the pile damp like a wrung-out sponge. If it smells sour, add dry browns and mix it. If it looks dusty and dry, sprinkle in a little water. Turn outdoor compost every week or two with a garden fork to add air.

Compost is ready when it looks dark, smells earthy, and you can no longer recognize most scraps. Use it in pots, garden beds, or around houseplants. Start small. The habit matters more than the perfect setup.
