A long walk teaches me how noisy I am before the world ever says a word.

For the first mile, I argue with people who are not there. I rewrite emails. I defend myself against charges no one has made. My body is moving, but my mind is still indoors, pacing the same narrow room.

Then the road starts doing its quiet work. A dog barks behind a fence. A maple drops one red leaf onto the sidewalk like it has been waiting for my attention. My breathing finds a steady rhythm, and the problems I brought with me lose their sharp edges. They do not disappear. That would be too easy. But they become objects I can hold instead of weather I have to live inside.

A long walk also teaches scale. The hill that looks gentle from a car is rude on foot. The house at the end of the block is farther away than I think. My own sadness, which felt enormous at the kitchen table, becomes one thing among many: cracked pavement, late sun, someone cooking onions, a child’s chalk drawing half erased by rain.

By the time I turn back, I am rarely fixed. I am simply returned to myself, a little less convinced that thinking harder is the same as living well.
