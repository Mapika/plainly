**What a Long Walk Teaches**

The first hour is usually wasted. You're still chewing on whatever you brought with you — an argument, a deadline, the email you didn't send. Your feet move but your head is back at the desk.

Then something shifts. Around mile four, give or take, the noise quiets down. Not because you've solved anything. You just get tired of holding it.

This is the part nobody tells you about walking. It isn't romantic. Your socks bunch. A blister starts on your right heel and you keep going anyway, because turning back would take just as long. You notice a fence post leaning at a strange angle. A dog watches you from a porch and decides you're not worth barking at.

What I've learned, walking long distances alone, is that most of my problems are smaller than my attention makes them. Give a worry six miles of road and it tends to shrink. Not disappear — shrink. It becomes the size it actually is, instead of the size it was when I was sitting still.

I keep walking because I keep forgetting this. The lesson doesn't stick. That's fine. The road is still there.
