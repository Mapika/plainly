Open-plan offices, for all their supposed benefits, are a bad idea. Proponents often tout collaboration and a sense of community, but the reality is far messier. Instead of fostering connection, they create a constant hum of distraction. Imagine trying to concentrate on a complex report while someone loudly recounts their weekend, or a colleague takes a particularly animated sales call a few feet away. It’s an auditory assault.

Privacy, both visual and aural, is a casualty. People feel exposed, leading to a reluctance to make private calls or engage in focused, deep work. Studies consistently show a drop in face-to-face interaction, not an increase, as employees resort to email or instant messaging to avoid bothering their neighbors. The initial cost savings might appeal to management, but the long-term impact on productivity and employee well-being far outweighs any perceived financial advantage. We need spaces that support different work styles, not a one-size-fits-all solution that inevitably suits no one well.
