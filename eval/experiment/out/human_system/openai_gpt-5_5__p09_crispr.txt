CRISPR is a gene-editing tool that lets scientists make targeted changes to DNA, the instruction code inside living cells. The name comes from a natural defense system found in bacteria. Bacteria use CRISPR to recognize and cut genetic material from viruses that attack them. Researchers adapted that system into a tool for editing genes.

The best-known version uses two main parts: a guide RNA and a cutting protein, often Cas9. The guide RNA is designed to match a specific stretch of DNA. It leads Cas9 to that exact location in the genome, much like a search term finds a phrase in a long document. When Cas9 gets there, it cuts both strands of DNA.

The cell then tries to repair the break. That repair step is where editing happens. If scientists want to disable a gene, they can let the cell patch the cut imperfectly, which often disrupts the gene. If they want to change a sequence, they can sometimes provide a DNA template for the cell to copy during repair.

CRISPR is powerful because it is relatively cheap, fast, and precise compared with older gene-editing methods. It is used in research, crop breeding, and experimental treatments for genetic diseases. But precision is not perfect, and editing embryos or inherited DNA raises serious ethical questions.
