I remember walking home from school as a kid, a mile or so through suburban streets. It felt like an eternity then, a forced march. Now, I seek out long walks, especially when I feel stuck. Not a quick loop around the block, but a proper journey.

There's something about the rhythm of putting one foot in front of the other, hour after hour, that strips away the noise. My mind, usually a jumble of to-do lists and anxieties, slowly unwinds. I start to notice things: the way the light hits a particular tree, the intricate pattern of a drain cover, the faint scent of rain on dry pavement. These details, usually invisible, suddenly stand out.

Problems that seemed insurmountable shrink in scale. It's not that the answers magically appear, but the frantic urgency around them dissipates. I gain perspective. A long walk teaches you that progress, however slow, is still progress. You don't have to sprint; you just have to keep moving. And sometimes, just sometimes, the best way to move forward is to simply walk.
