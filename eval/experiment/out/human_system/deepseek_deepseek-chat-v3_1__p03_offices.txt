Open-plan offices are a failure sold as a virtue. Designed to foster collaboration, they instead manufacture distraction. The constant low-grade hum of conversations, phone calls, and keyboard clatter isn’t a soundtrack to productivity; it’s cognitive sabotage. It forces workers into a defensive posture, donning noise-canceling headphones to create the very privacy the layout destroyed.

The promised serendipitous collisions are rare. Most daily work requires focus, not forced interaction. What you get instead is a loss of agency—no space to think aloud, have a sensitive call, or simply retreat. The model treats employees like interchangeable parts in a machine, prioritizing real estate savings over human needs. It confuses visibility with productivity.

True collaboration requires both coming together and pulling apart. The best workplaces offer choice: quiet rooms for deep work, meeting spaces for teams, and yes, some open areas for quick chats. The open-plan monopoly sacrifices this essential balance on the altar of cost-cutting and a misguided cult of transparency. We traded walls for headphones, and called it progress.
