Last week, I walked fifteen miles through the Berkshires with nothing but a water bottle and restless thoughts. The first few miles were about escape—leaving behind cluttered emails, pending tasks, the low hum of digital noise. Then something shifted.

Walking transforms thinking. Your body settles into rhythm, and the mind starts to wander differently—not frantically, but with a kind of liquid intelligence. Each step becomes a small negotiation with landscape and self.

I noticed subtle things: how lichen clings to granite in delicate green networks, how a hawk's shadow moves across open fields faster than the bird itself. My pace slowed. My breathing deepened. The internal chatter that usually fills my head became quiet background noise.

By mile ten, my feet ached. My shoulders carried tension from the backpack. But my mind felt clear, almost transparent. Walking strips away pretense. You confront your actual physical limits, your real capacities for endurance.

When I finally reached my destination, I wasn't just tired. I was recalibrated. The walk had reminded me that movement isn't just physical—it's a form of meditation, a way of listening to yourself without judgment. Sometimes you need to put distance between yourself and your usual patterns to understand them.
