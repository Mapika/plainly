Most vaccines work by introducing a weakened or inactivated pathogen to train the immune system. mRNA vaccines take a radical different approach, using a genetic messenger to essentially turn our own cells into temporary vaccine factories.

At the core of this technology is messenger RNA (mRNA), a molecule that carries genetic instructions from DNA to cellular protein-making machinery. In an mRNA vaccine, scientists synthesize a specific strand of mRNA that contains instructions for making a distinctive protein from the target virus — in COVID-19's case, the spike protein.

When the vaccine is injected, the mRNA enters human cells and triggers those cells to produce the viral protein. This isn't the whole virus, just a crucial piece of it. The immune system quickly recognizes this protein as foreign and mounts a defense, creating antibodies and training specialized T-cells to recognize and fight the real virus if it ever enters the body.

The mRNA itself is fragile and temporary. It doesn't enter the cell nucleus, can't interact with our DNA, and breaks down naturally within days. This means the protein production is short-lived, but the immune memory remains.

This approach offers significant advantages: faster development, greater precision, and the ability to quickly modify vaccines in response to viral mutations. It's a breakthrough that could transform how we respond to infectious diseases.
