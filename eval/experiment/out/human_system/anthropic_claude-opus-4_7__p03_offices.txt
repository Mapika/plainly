**The Open-Plan Office Was a Mistake**

I've worked in three of them. Each was sold the same way: collaboration, energy, the spontaneous hallway chat that becomes a million-dollar idea. What I actually got was a man two desks over who took every call on speakerphone and a colleague who clipped her nails on Wednesdays.

The research is fairly damning. A Harvard study in 2018 found that when companies switched to open layouts, face-to-face interaction dropped by about 70%. People put on headphones and retreated to Slack. The thing the design was meant to encourage was the thing it killed.

There's a class element too, which nobody likes to mention. Executives still get offices with doors. Junior staff get the long table and the hot desk and the booking app that crashes on Monday mornings. Open-plan is egalitarian only if you ignore who actually has to live in it.

I'll grant one point: real estate is expensive, and walls cost money. Fine. Say that. Don't dress up a cost-cutting decision as a philosophy of innovation. Give people a quiet room when they need to think, a meeting room when they need to talk, and stop pretending a noisy warehouse is a feature.
