Open-plan offices are a bad bargain. They look efficient on a floor plan, but they often waste the one thing office workers need most: attention.

The promise is easy to understand. Put everyone in the same room and people will talk more, solve problems faster, and feel less boxed in. Sometimes that happens. A designer can lean over to ask an engineer a quick question. A new employee can pick up habits by listening. The room can feel alive.

But most work is not a team huddle. It is reading a hard email, fixing a spreadsheet, writing code, checking a contract, or thinking through a decision before speaking. In an open office, all of that happens beside phone calls, side chats, loud keyboards, snack wrappers, and someone’s 2 p.m. laugh. Headphones become walls. Slack replaces speech. People book meeting rooms just to be alone.

The worst part is that open offices shift the burden onto the worker. If you cannot focus, you are told to manage your time better, wear noise-canceling headphones, or work from home. The design gets a pass.

A better office has a mix: quiet rooms, small team spaces, real doors, and shared areas for conversation. Open space can have a place. It should not be the default.
