# The Silk Road: An Overview

The Silk Road was a vast network of trade routes that connected the East and West for over 1,500 years, stretching roughly 4,000 miles from China to the Mediterranean. Established during China's Han Dynasty around 130 BCE, when Emperor Wu sent envoy Zhang Qian to Central Asia, the route flourished until the 15th century, when maritime trade routes gradually replaced overland travel.

Named for the lucrative silk trade originating in China, the network actually facilitated the exchange of countless goods, including spices, precious metals, gemstones, glassware, textiles, and horses. Major hub cities like Samarkand, Bukhara, and Kashgar grew wealthy and cosmopolitan as caravans passed through.

The Silk Road's significance extended far beyond commerce. It served as a powerful conduit for cultural, religious, and technological exchange. Buddhism spread from India into China, while Islam later traveled eastward along these routes. Innovations such as papermaking, gunpowder, and printing moved westward, while artistic styles, languages, and scientific knowledge flowed in both directions. Tragically, the routes also transmitted diseases, most notably the Black Death in the 14th century.

Today, the Silk Road remains a powerful symbol of globalization, cross-cultural connection, and humanity's enduring drive to explore and exchange.
