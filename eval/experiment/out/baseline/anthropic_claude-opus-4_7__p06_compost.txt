# A Beginner's Guide to Composting at Home

Composting is a simple way to turn kitchen scraps and yard waste into nutrient-rich soil for your garden—while reducing what you send to the landfill.

**Getting Started**

Choose a bin that fits your space. Outdoor options include tumblers or simple wooden bins, while apartment dwellers can use countertop compost containers or worm bins.

**What to Add**

Successful composting requires a balance of two ingredients:
- **Greens (nitrogen):** fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells, fresh grass clippings
- **Browns (carbon):** dry leaves, shredded cardboard, newspaper, straw

Aim for roughly 3 parts browns to 1 part greens.

**What to Avoid**

Skip meat, dairy, oily foods, and pet waste, as these can attract pests and create odors.

**Maintaining Your Pile**

Keep your compost as moist as a wrung-out sponge, and turn it with a shovel or pitchfork every week or two to add oxygen. This speeds up decomposition and prevents bad smells.

**Harvesting**

In two to six months, your compost will look dark, crumbly, and smell earthy. Mix it into garden beds, sprinkle it on your lawn, or use it for potted plants.

Start small, stay consistent, and enjoy the rewards!
