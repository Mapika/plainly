mRNA vaccines represent a revolutionary approach to immunization. Unlike traditional vaccines that introduce a weakened or inactive virus, mRNA vaccines deliver a genetic blueprint – messenger RNA (mRNA) – to our cells. This mRNA carries instructions for making a specific, harmless piece of a pathogen, typically the spike protein of a virus like SARS-CoV-2.

Once inside our cells, the cellular machinery reads these instructions and produces the spike protein. Crucially, this protein is *not* infectious and cannot cause disease. Our immune system then recognizes this foreign protein as an invader and mounts a protective response, generating antibodies and T-cells. If we later encounter the actual virus, our immune system is already primed to quickly neutralize it, preventing illness. The mRNA itself is fragile and quickly degrades after delivering its message, leaving no lasting trace in the body. This innovative technology offers rapid development and production, making it a powerful tool in combating infectious diseases.
