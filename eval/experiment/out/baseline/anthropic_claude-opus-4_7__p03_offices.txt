# The Open-Plan Office Experiment Has Failed

Open-plan offices were sold to us as a revolution: tear down the walls, and collaboration, creativity, and camaraderie would flourish. Two decades on, the evidence suggests the opposite.

A landmark Harvard study found that when companies switched to open layouts, face-to-face interaction actually *dropped* by around 70%, while email and messaging surged. Workers, overwhelmed by noise and constant visibility, retreated into headphones and digital silos. Far from sparking spontaneous brainstorms, the open plan often produces a strange, performative quiet punctuated by distraction.

The costs are real. Studies link open offices to higher stress, more sick days, and reduced productivity, particularly for tasks requiring deep focus. Introverts suffer disproportionately, but no one truly thrives when every phone call becomes a public broadcast.

Of course, the appeal to employers is obvious: open plans are cheap, flexible, and project an image of modern egalitarianism. But cramming people together is not the same as bringing them together.

The better model is a hybrid one — quiet zones for focused work, comfortable spaces for genuine collaboration, and the option to retreat when needed. Offices should be designed around how people actually work, not around how managers wish they would.
