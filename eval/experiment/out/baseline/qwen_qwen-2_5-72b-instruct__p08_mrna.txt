mRNA vaccines represent a groundbreaking approach to immunization, harnessing the body’s own cellular machinery to fight disease. Unlike traditional vaccines, which often use weakened or inactivated pathogens, mRNA vaccines contain a synthetic version of messenger RNA (mRNA), a molecule that carries instructions for making proteins. 

When an mRNA vaccine is injected, the mRNA enters cells and directs them to produce a specific protein, typically a harmless piece of the virus the vaccine is targeting, such as the spike protein of the SARS-CoV-2 virus. The cells then display this protein on their surface, triggering an immune response. The immune system recognizes the foreign protein and begins to produce antibodies and activate T-cells to fight it. This response creates a memory in the immune system, so if the actual virus later invades the body, the immune system is primed and ready to neutralize it quickly.

The mRNA itself is quickly degraded by the body, leaving no trace, and the vaccine does not interact with or alter the recipient’s DNA. This technology allows for rapid vaccine development and production, making it a powerful tool in the fight against infectious diseases.
