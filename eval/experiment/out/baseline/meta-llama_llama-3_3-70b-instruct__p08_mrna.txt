mRNA vaccines are a type of vaccine that uses a piece of genetic material called messenger RNA (mRNA) to instruct cells in the body to produce a specific protein. This protein is a harmless piece of a virus, such as the spike protein found on the surface of the SARS-CoV-2 virus that causes COVID-19. When the mRNA is introduced into the body, it is taken up by cells, which then use the instructions to produce the protein. The immune system recognizes the protein as foreign and mounts an immune response, producing antibodies and immune cells that can recognize and attack the virus if it were to infect the body in the future.

The mRNA itself does not enter the cell's nucleus, where DNA is stored, and does not alter the body's genetic code. Instead, it remains in the cell's cytoplasm, where it is translated into protein and then broken down by the cell. The immune response generated by the vaccine provides long-term protection against infection, and the mRNA is degraded and eliminated by the body within a few days. This technology has been shown to be highly effective in clinical trials and has been used to develop vaccines against COVID-19 and other diseases.
