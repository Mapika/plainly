**CRISPR Gene Editing Explained**

CRISPR is a revolutionary gene-editing technology that allows scientists to precisely alter an organism's DNA. Often described as "genetic scissors," it offers the potential to correct genetic defects, treat diseases, and improve crops.

It works by harnessing a natural defense mechanism found in bacteria. When a virus attacks, bacteria capture snippets of the virus's DNA and store them in their own genome in a repetitive pattern called **CRISPR** (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats). This acts like a genetic "most wanted" poster.

If the virus attacks again, the bacteria use a special enzyme, most commonly **Cas9**, as molecular scissors. They create an RNA copy of the viral DNA "mugshot" (called a guide RNA) that leads the Cas9 scissors directly to the matching viral DNA sequence. Cas9 then cuts the invading DNA, disabling the virus.

Scientists have co-opted this system. They can design a synthetic guide RNA to lead the Cas9 enzyme to any specific gene in an organism's genome. Once Cas9 makes a cut, the cell's natural repair mechanisms kick in. Scientists can use this opportunity to disable a harmful gene or insert a new, healthy DNA sequence provided by them, effectively rewriting the genetic code with unprecedented precision.
