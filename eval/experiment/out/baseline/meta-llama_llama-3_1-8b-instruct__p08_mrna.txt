Here's an explainer on how mRNA vaccines work:

mRNA (messenger RNA) vaccines are a relatively new technology that uses a piece of genetic material to instruct our cells to produce a specific protein, which in turn triggers an immune response. Here's how they work:

When an mRNA vaccine is administered, it enters the cells of the body, where it is read by a cellular machinery called the ribosome. The ribosome translates the mRNA instructions into a specific protein, which is then displayed on the surface of the cell. This protein is the target of the immune response, and our immune system recognizes it as foreign.

The immune system responds by sending out immune cells, such as T-cells and B-cells, to attack the protein. This process is called immune priming. The immune cells remember the protein and can recognize it if it enters the body again in the future, allowing for a rapid and effective response to infection.

mRNA vaccines are particularly useful because they can be designed to target a wide range of diseases, including infectious diseases such as COVID-19, and even some types of cancer. They also have several advantages over traditional vaccines, including the ability to be rapidly manufactured and distributed, and the potential to provide long-term protection against disease.
