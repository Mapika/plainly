Open-plan offices are often promoted as spaces that encourage collaboration, creativity, and a sense of team spirit. In theory, removing walls makes it easier for employees to share ideas, ask quick questions, and feel connected to their colleagues. For some kinds of work—especially fast-moving projects that depend on constant communication—this can be useful.

However, I think open-plan offices are usually more harmful than helpful. The biggest problem is noise. Even small conversations, phone calls, or keyboard sounds can make it difficult to concentrate. Many jobs require deep focus, and an open office often forces workers to spend extra energy blocking out distractions. This can reduce productivity and increase stress.

There is also the issue of privacy. Employees may feel uncomfortable taking important calls, discussing sensitive matters, or simply having a quiet moment to think. In response, many people use headphones all day, which ironically reduces the collaboration open-plan offices are supposed to create.

A better approach would be a flexible office design: shared areas for teamwork, but also quiet rooms and private spaces for focused work. Open-plan offices are not a bad idea in every situation, but as a default model, they fail to respect how differently people work.
