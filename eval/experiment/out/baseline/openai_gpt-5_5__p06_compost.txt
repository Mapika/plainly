Starting composting at home is an easy way to reduce kitchen waste and create nutrient-rich material for your garden or houseplants.

First, choose a composting method. If you have outdoor space, use a compost bin, pile, or tumbler in a shaded, well-drained area. If you live in an apartment, consider a small indoor compost bin or a worm composting system.

Next, learn what to add. Compost needs a balance of “greens” and “browns.” Greens are moist, nitrogen-rich items like fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and grass clippings. Browns are dry, carbon-rich materials like dried leaves, shredded paper, cardboard, and small twigs. A good rule is to add about two to three parts browns for every one part greens.

Avoid adding meat, dairy, oily foods, pet waste, or diseased plants, as these can attract pests or cause odors.

Keep your compost slightly damp, like a wrung-out sponge. Turn or mix it every week or two to add air and help materials break down faster. If it smells bad, add more browns and mix it. If it seems too dry, add a little water or more greens.

Compost is ready when it looks dark, crumbly, and earthy. Use it to enrich soil, top-dress plants, or mix into garden beds.
