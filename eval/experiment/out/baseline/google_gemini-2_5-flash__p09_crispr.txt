CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) gene editing is a revolutionary technology that allows scientists to precisely modify DNA. Think of it as a pair of molecular scissors that can cut out, replace, or insert specific DNA sequences.

It works by using a guide RNA molecule that's designed to match a target DNA sequence. This guide RNA then leads a special enzyme, Cas9 (or similar enzymes), to the exact location on the DNA. Once there, Cas9 makes a precise cut in the DNA. The cell then tries to repair this break, and scientists can either disable the gene, insert new genetic material, or correct faulty sequences during this repair process. This targeted approach makes CRISPR incredibly powerful for correcting genetic defects, studying gene function, and potentially treating a wide range of diseases.
