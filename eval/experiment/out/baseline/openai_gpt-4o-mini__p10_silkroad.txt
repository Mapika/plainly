The Silk Road was a network of trade routes that connected the East and West, flourishing from around the 2nd century BCE to the 14th century CE. Established during the Han Dynasty, it facilitated not only the exchange of goods such as silk, spices, and precious metals but also the spread of ideas, culture, and innovations. The name "Silk Road" derives from the lucrative silk trade that was highly coveted in the West.

As various empires rose and fell, including the Roman Empire, the Byzantine Empire, and the Mongol Empire, the Silk Road adapted, linking diverse civilizations across Asia, the Middle East, and Europe. This exchange led to significant cultural interactions, including the dissemination of religions such as Buddhism, Zoroastrianism, and Islam, as well as advancements in technology and science.

The Silk Road’s significance extends beyond commerce; it played a crucial role in shaping the cultural and social landscapes of the regions it connected. Its decline began in the late medieval period due to the rise of maritime trade routes and political instability. Nevertheless, the legacy of the Silk Road endures, highlighting the enduring connections between different cultures and the early globalization of trade and ideas.
