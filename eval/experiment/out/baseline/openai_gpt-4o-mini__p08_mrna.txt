mRNA vaccines represent a groundbreaking approach to immunization, utilizing messenger RNA (mRNA) to instruct cells in the body to produce a harmless piece of the virus that causes a specific disease, such as COVID-19. Unlike traditional vaccines, which often contain weakened or inactivated forms of the virus, mRNA vaccines do not use the live virus that causes the illness.

When an mRNA vaccine is administered, it delivers synthetic mRNA into the body’s cells. This mRNA contains the genetic instructions for making a viral protein, typically the spike protein found on the surface of the virus. The cells read this mRNA and produce the viral protein, which is then displayed on their surfaces. This prompts the immune system to recognize the protein as foreign, triggering an immune response.

The immune system produces antibodies and activates T-cells to fight the infection. If the vaccinated individual later encounters the actual virus, their immune system is already primed to recognize and combat it effectively, thus providing protection against the disease. Importantly, mRNA does not alter a person’s DNA, as it remains in the cytoplasm of the cell and is broken down shortly after the protein is made. This innovative technology has proven to be a vital tool in controlling infectious diseases.
