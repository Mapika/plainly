The Silk Road was an ancient network of trade routes that connected the East and West, stretching from the Chinese capital of Chang'an to the Mediterranean Sea. Formally established during the Han Dynasty in the 2nd century BCE, its use ebbed and flowed for over 1,500 years. While silk was its most famous commodity, giving the route its 19th-century name, the road facilitated the exchange of countless other goods, including spices, gold, glassware, and ideas.

Its significance, however, extends far beyond material trade. The Silk Road was a crucial conduit for the cross-cultural exchange that shaped world history. It enabled the spread of technologies like papermaking and gunpowder from China to the West. Most profoundly, it served as a highway for religions, allowing Buddhism to travel from India to East Asia, and Christianity and Islam to spread eastward. This interconnected system of routes linked the great civilizations of China, India, Persia, Arabia, and the Roman Empire, fostering an unprecedented level of cultural and intellectual diffusion that laid the groundwork for the modern globalized world.
