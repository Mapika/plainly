# How mRNA Vaccines Work

Traditional vaccines typically introduce a weakened or inactivated virus to train your immune system. mRNA vaccines take a fundamentally different approach: instead of delivering the pathogen itself, they deliver instructions.

Messenger RNA (mRNA) is a naturally occurring molecule that your cells use every day to translate genetic information from DNA into proteins. mRNA vaccines harness this process. The vaccine contains a small piece of synthetic mRNA encoding a single, harmless protein from the target virus—for COVID-19 vaccines, this is the distinctive "spike" protein found on the coronavirus's surface. The mRNA is wrapped in a tiny lipid (fat) bubble that protects it and helps it enter your cells.

Once inside, your cells read the mRNA and temporarily produce the spike protein, displaying it on their surface. Your immune system recognizes this protein as foreign and mounts a response, generating antibodies and training immune cells to recognize it. If you later encounter the actual virus, your body is primed to attack it quickly.

Importantly, mRNA never enters the cell's nucleus and cannot alter your DNA. It also degrades within days, leaving behind only the immune memory. This platform is remarkably flexible: once scientists know a pathogen's genetic sequence, new vaccines can be designed in weeks rather than years.
