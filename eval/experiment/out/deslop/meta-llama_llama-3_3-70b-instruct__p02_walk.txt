A long walk can teach you things a conversation never will. Setting out, I'm usually restless, but the rhythm of footsteps quiets that noise and pulls my attention inward. The early energy fades after a few miles, and what replaces it is more honest — a clearer sense of where my body ends and my willpower begins. Exhaustion strips away the performance, and whatever remains is closer to the truth of who I am.

The world also sharpens as the walk stretches on. I start catching details I'd blown past before: the way light hits a particular wall, the smell of cut grass two streets over, a bird I can hear but can't locate. My mind stops generating its usual static. There's something almost meditative about sustained movement — not in any mystical sense, but in the plain fact that the body's occupation frees the mind to settle.

What I take home isn't a conclusion so much as a recalibration. The pace of a walk resists urgency, and spending hours inside that pace is a reminder that most things don't actually require rushing. Putting one foot in front of the other sounds trivial until you've done it for three hours through fatigue and boredom and come out the other side still moving. That's not a metaphor — it's just a useful thing to have done.
