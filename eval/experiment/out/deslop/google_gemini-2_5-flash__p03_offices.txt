The open-plan office, billed as a modern marvel of collaboration, often amounts to a cost-cutting exercise dressed up in progressive language. Proponents claim it sparks spontaneous interaction and flattens hierarchies, but for many workers the daily reality is noise. Chatter, phone calls, and keyboard clatter make sustained concentration nearly impossible. Privacy gets sacrificed for visibility, and the result is chronic stress and the exhausting sense of being permanently watched.

A well-designed open space—with quiet zones and decent acoustics—can reduce some of these problems. But most implementations skip that part, trading human psychology for square footage. Anyone doing work that demands real focus, or anyone who simply recharges alone rather than in a crowd, will find the open-plan actively hostile to getting things done. The vision of a buzzing collaborative hub sounds appealing on a slide deck. In practice, it leaves a lot of people quietly wishing they had a door to close.
