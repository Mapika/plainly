Why You Should Read More Books

Most people scroll through their phones for hours each day but haven't finished a book in months. That's worth reconsidering.

Reading builds knowledge in a way that news feeds and short videos can't. A single book on history or science goes deeper than a hundred articles, and fiction does something different still—it puts you inside other lives, other times, other ways of seeing. That kind of sustained immersion changes how you think.

There are real mental health benefits too. Studies show reading lowers cortisol levels more quickly than listening to music or taking a walk. It also demands the kind of focused attention that strengthens memory and may slow cognitive decline later in life. A phone offers the opposite: constant interruption, shallow engagement, the vague dissatisfaction of having consumed a lot while retaining almost nothing.

Books also do something television can't. Because the images exist only in your head, your brain does the work of constructing them—and that exercise builds both creativity and empathy. Readers who inhabit a character's perspective for three hundred pages come away with a more nuanced understanding of people unlike themselves.

And books are cheap. A library card costs nothing. Used copies are a dollar or two. You can read on a bus, in a waiting room, before sleep.

None of this requires a dramatic lifestyle change. Start with thirty pages a night. The returns are larger than you'd expect.
