Open-plan offices are often promoted as spaces that encourage collaboration, creativity, and team spirit. In theory, removing walls makes it easier for employees to share ideas, ask quick questions, and feel connected to their colleagues. For some kinds of work—especially fast-moving projects that depend on constant communication—this can be useful.

However, open-plan offices are usually more harmful than helpful. The biggest problem is noise. Even small conversations, phone calls, or keyboard sounds can make it difficult to concentrate. Many jobs require deep focus, and an open office forces workers to spend extra energy blocking out distractions, which reduces productivity and raises stress levels.

There is also the issue of privacy. Employees may feel uncomfortable taking important calls or discussing sensitive matters. Many end up wearing headphones all day, which ironically kills the collaboration these offices are supposed to create.

A better approach would be a flexible design: shared areas for teamwork alongside quiet rooms and private spaces for focused work. Open-plan offices are not wrong in every situation, but as a default model they fail to respect how differently people work.
