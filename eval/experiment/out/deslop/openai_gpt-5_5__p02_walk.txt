A long walk teaches me that most things do not need to be solved as quickly as I think they do. At the start, I carry my worries like a heavy backpack: messages I haven't answered, decisions I've delayed, small regrets that grow louder when I sit still. But after a mile or two, my thoughts begin to match the rhythm of my feet. The world widens. I notice the slant of light on a fence, the stubborn weed pushing through a crack in the sidewalk, the way strangers move through their own private weather.

Walking reminds me that progress can be quiet. There is no dramatic leap from one place to another, only step after step, yet the distance changes. A hill that looked difficult from far away becomes manageable when taken slowly. That lesson follows me home: large problems are rarely conquered all at once. They are crossed in small, steady movements.

A long walk also teaches humility. My mind is not the whole world. Trees keep growing, birds keep calling, clouds keep passing whether I am worried or not. By the time I return, I am usually not transformed, but I am softened — reminded that I am part of something larger, and that sometimes the best way forward is simply to keep moving.
