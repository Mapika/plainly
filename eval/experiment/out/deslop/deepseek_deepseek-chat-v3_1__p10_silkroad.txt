The Silk Road was an ancient network of trade routes connecting East and West, stretching from the Chinese capital of Chang'an to the Mediterranean Sea. Formally established during the Han Dynasty in the 2nd century BCE, it remained in use for over 1,500 years. Silk was its most famous commodity — the source of its 19th-century name — but merchants also carried spices, gold, glassware, and ideas.

Its importance ran deeper than commerce. The Silk Road carried technologies westward out of China, among them papermaking and gunpowder. It functioned as a highway for religion: Buddhism traveled from India into East Asia along its corridors, while Christianity and Islam pushed eastward. By linking China, India, Persia, Arabia, and Rome, the routes produced a sustained exchange of culture and knowledge between civilizations that might otherwise have developed in near-total isolation, and that exchange left a permanent mark on the world that followed.
