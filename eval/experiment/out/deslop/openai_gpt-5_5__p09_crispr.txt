CRISPR is a gene-editing tool that lets scientists make precise changes to DNA in living cells. The name comes from a defense system bacteria use to recognize and cut the DNA of invading viruses. Researchers adapted this system to edit genes in plants, animals, and human cells.

The most common version uses two components: a guide RNA and a cutting enzyme called Cas9. The guide RNA is designed to match a specific DNA sequence, directing Cas9 to the right location in the genome. Once there, Cas9 cuts both strands of the DNA.

The cell then attempts to repair the break, and scientists can exploit this process in different ways. Sometimes the repair introduces small errors that effectively disable a gene. Other times, researchers supply a new DNA template so the cell incorporates a corrected sequence instead.

CRISPR has applications in research, agriculture, and medicine. It could help treat genetic diseases and produce crops resistant to disease, and it gives scientists a way to study what individual genes actually do. Because it can alter living organisms—human embryos included—it also raises serious ethical and safety questions that remain unresolved.
