**The Case for Reading Books**

Screens dominate how most people consume information now, and books have quietly lost ground. That's a shame, because reading does things other media simply don't.

For a start, it builds cognitive muscle. Working through a dense argument or a layered narrative forces the brain to slow down and pay attention. Vocabulary grows almost incidentally, and sustained engagement with complex characters tends to sharpen empathy in ways that a three-minute video rarely achieves. Readers who encounter lives very different from their own often find it easier to understand people in real situations too.

There's also the stress question. A University of Sussex study found that six minutes of reading reduced participants' heart rates more effectively than a walk or listening to music. The mechanism is simple: a compelling story demands enough concentration to crowd out whatever was worrying you.

Books are also one of the most efficient ways to absorb hard-won knowledge. A good non-fiction title can compress decades of research or experience into a few hundred pages. Fiction, meanwhile, offers something different — a way into cultures, historical moments, and moral questions that dry summaries can't replicate.

The practical barrier is time, and it's real. But even twenty minutes before bed, replacing a scroll through social media, adds up to roughly a book a month. That's twelve books a year, each one capable of changing how you think about something. The return on that investment is difficult to match.
