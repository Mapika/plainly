mRNA vaccines give your cells temporary instructions for making a harmless piece of a pathogen—usually a protein the immune system can recognize. The mRNA is packaged in tiny fat-like particles called lipid nanoparticles, which protect it and help it enter cells after injection.

Once inside the cytoplasm, ribosomes read the mRNA and produce the target protein—the spike protein, in the case of some COVID-19 vaccines. This protein cannot cause disease, but it signals the immune system that something foreign is present.

The immune system then mounts a response. B cells learn to recognize the protein and produce antibodies. T cells help coordinate the response and can destroy infected cells if the real pathogen appears later. Some of these cells become memory cells, enabling a faster, stronger response to future exposure.

The mRNA never enters the nucleus and cannot alter DNA. It breaks down within days. What remains is immune memory.
