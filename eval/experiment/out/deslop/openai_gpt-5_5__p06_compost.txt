Starting a compost pile at home is one of the simplest ways to cut kitchen waste and produce rich material for your garden or houseplants.

First, pick a method. Outdoor space opens up options like a bin, pile, or tumbler—place it somewhere shaded with decent drainage. In an apartment, a small countertop bin or a worm composting setup works well.

Compost needs a balance of "greens" and "browns." Greens are moist, nitrogen-rich scraps: fruit and vegetable peels, coffee grounds, grass clippings. Browns are dry and carbon-rich: dead leaves, shredded cardboard, small twigs. Aim for roughly two to three parts browns for every one part greens.

Leave out meat, dairy, oily foods, pet waste, and diseased plants. These attract pests and create odors.

Keep the pile about as damp as a wrung-out sponge, and turn it every week or two so air can circulate and break things down faster. If it smells, add more browns and mix it. If it looks dry, add water or a handful of greens.

Finished compost is dark, crumbly, and smells like soil. Spread it over garden beds, work it into potting mix, or use it to top-dress plants.
