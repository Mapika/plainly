A long walk is a quiet conversation with yourself, marked by the rhythm of your steps. The initial burst of energy settles into a steady cadence, and the mind begins to unfold. Distractions recede. The phone stays in your pocket, the to-do list forgotten.

Clarity surfaces in that space. A problem that felt insurmountable suddenly shows a way through. A creative block loosens, and new ideas rise like bubbles in a stream. Moving forward physically seems to move something mentally too. You learn to put one foot in front of the other when the destination still feels far off. You start noticing things you'd otherwise walk past — the pattern of a leaf, the smell of rain on dry earth, the way the light shifts between trees.

For me, a long walk is an exercise in patience and attention. It strips away the noise and leaves you with something harder to find at a desk: the sense that if you just keep moving, you'll eventually work it out.
