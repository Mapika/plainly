# The Silk Road: An Overview

The Silk Road was a vast network of trade routes connecting East and West for over 1,500 years, stretching roughly 4,000 miles from China to the Mediterranean. It was established during China's Han Dynasty around 130 BCE, when Emperor Wu sent envoy Zhang Qian to Central Asia, and flourished until the 15th century, when maritime routes gradually displaced overland travel.

Named for the lucrative silk trade originating in China, the network carried far more than fabric — spices, precious metals, gemstones, glassware, and horses all moved along its paths. Hub cities like Samarkand, Bukhara, and Kashgar grew wealthy and cosmopolitan as caravans passed through.

Commerce was only part of the story. Buddhism spread from India into China along these routes, and Islam later traveled eastward the same way. Papermaking, gunpowder, and printing moved westward, while artistic styles, languages, and scientific knowledge crossed in both directions. The routes also carried disease. The Black Death reached Europe in the 14th century by traveling westward through the same networks that carried silk and spices.

The Silk Road endures as a symbol of how deeply interconnected the ancient world already was — and how much of what we consider distinctly Eastern or Western actually passed through someone else's hands first.
