The Silk Road—a name coined in the 19th century—was never a single road. It was a vast network of ancient trade routes linking East and West for over 1,500 years, beginning around 200 BCE, carrying goods, ideas, religions, and technologies in both directions.

Silk was the prestige commodity, but the routes also moved spices, precious metals, textiles, and agricultural products. The cultural traffic mattered more. Buddhism, Christianity, and Islam all spread along these pathways, reshaping the spiritual life of entire continents. Paper-making, gunpowder, and printing moved westward; glassmaking and new agricultural techniques traveled east.

Maritime trade routes emerged in the 15th century and gradually drew commerce away from the overland paths. But the Silk Road's deeper mark is hard to overstate: centuries before anyone spoke of globalization, Eurasian civilizations were already deeply entangled with one another, and much of what we think of as distinctly regional—in religion, technology, and culture—arrived from somewhere else along those dusty, dangerous roads.
