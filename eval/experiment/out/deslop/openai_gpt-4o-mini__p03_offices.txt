Open-plan offices are widely promoted as engines of collaboration and creativity, but the evidence tells a different story. Stripping away walls rarely produces the teamwork advocates promise.

The most immediate problem is noise. Conversations, phone calls, and general office din make sustained concentration difficult, and research links these environments to higher stress and lower job satisfaction. Privacy is essentially gone—employees have nowhere to retreat when they need to think.

The design also assumes everyone works the same way. Some people do their best thinking around others; many don't. When there's no quiet space available, the workers who need solitude simply underperform, and creativity suffers rather than flourishes.

A better model would mix open collaborative areas with enclosed private spaces, giving people genuine choice over where they work. Offices designed around that flexibility tend to produce better results than those built on a single, fashionable idea.
