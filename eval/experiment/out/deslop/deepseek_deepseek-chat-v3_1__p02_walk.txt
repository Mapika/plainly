I used to think a long walk was simply a way to get from one place to another. Now I know it is a way to arrive at yourself.

The first mile is always for shedding. The tightness in your shoulders, the mental clutter, the urgency of the inbox—they loosen with each step. Your breathing syncs with your stride. The noise inside quiets.

By the second mile, you begin to notice. The fractal pattern of a leaf. The way light filters through trees. The industrious path of an ant crossing the pavement. Your senses, dulled by screens and routine, sharpen. You are no longer just moving through the world; you are part of it.

The final lesson comes in the quiet exhaustion, when your mind has run out of things to churn over. In that emptied space, clarity emerges. Solutions to problems you've been wrestling with appear, unbidden. Or sometimes you simply remember what it feels like to *be*, without agenda or performance. You return home with dusty shoes and a lighter spirit, having found that forward motion, however slow, is its own kind of answer.
