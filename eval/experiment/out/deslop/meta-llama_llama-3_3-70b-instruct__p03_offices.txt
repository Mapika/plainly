Open-plan offices have spread rapidly across corporate America, with companies pitching them as engines of collaboration and creativity. The pitch doesn't hold up. Yes, they can build a sense of community, but they also make it genuinely hard to get work done.

Strip away private offices and you get background noise, constant interruptions, and colleagues wandering through your field of vision. Focus suffers, stress rises, and confidential conversations become nearly impossible — the kind you need to have with HR, or a client, or your manager about a performance issue.

The design also tends to favor extroverts. People who recharge in quiet, uninterrupted stretches find open offices actively draining rather than energizing. This isn't a personality quirk to be managed; it's a real difference in how people work best. The surge in remote work has made this obvious: given the choice, many employees will take a spare bedroom over a buzzing floor plan every time.

Companies don't need to pick between a sea of open desks and a maze of closed doors. Workplaces can offer both — quiet rooms for deep work, shared spaces for collaboration, and enough flexibility that people aren't forced into one mode all day. That kind of design actually delivers what open offices only promise.
