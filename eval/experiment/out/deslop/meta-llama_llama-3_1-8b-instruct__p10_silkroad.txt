The Silk Road was a network of ancient trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean, stretching over 4,000 miles across Central Asia. Established during the Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE), it served as the main artery for exchanging goods, ideas, and technologies between East and West. Merchants and travelers carried silk, spices, tea, and precious stones along its length.

The route shaped civilizations in concrete ways. Chinese silk was so prized in Rome that the Senate repeatedly tried to ban its import, fearing the drain on silver. In the other direction, Greek and Roman advances in astronomy, mathematics, and medicine reached China through Buddhist monks and traveling scholars. The road also carried religions—Buddhism, Islam, and Christianity all spread along its paths—and technologies like the compass and gunpowder moved westward, eventually transforming warfare and navigation in Europe.

The Silk Road's importance was more than commercial. It showed that distant civilizations were never as isolated as they appeared, and that contact between them produced changes neither side could have achieved alone. Today it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and its history still shapes how scholars and policymakers think about international exchange. The routes that once carried bolts of silk across deserts and mountain passes left a mark on language, religion, science, and politics that persists in the modern world.
