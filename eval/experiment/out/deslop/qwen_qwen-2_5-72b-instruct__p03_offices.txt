Open-plan offices have become common across many industries, with companies drawn to the promise of better collaboration and communication. The reality, though, is messier than the pitch.

The layout does have genuine benefits. Shared space can build a sense of community and make spontaneous teamwork easier, which suits certain kinds of work well. But it also produces noise and constant distraction. Research has linked open-plan environments to higher stress, lower job satisfaction, and increased sick days — outcomes that undercut the productivity gains companies were hoping for.

Privacy is another real problem. Employees struggle to hold confidential conversations or sustain the kind of deep focus that demanding work requires. This hits introverts and those handling sensitive projects especially hard.

Some companies have responded sensibly, adding private rooms or quiet zones where people can retreat when they need to concentrate. That flexibility matters. No single layout works for every person or every task, and an office that ignores that fact will cost the company in ways that don't always show up immediately on a balance sheet. The goal should be a workplace designed around what employees actually need, not around what looked good in an architectural magazine.
