The Silk Road was a network of trade routes connecting East and West, active from roughly the 2nd century BCE to the 14th century CE. Established during the Han Dynasty, it carried silk, spices, and precious metals alongside religions, ideas, and technologies. The name itself comes from the silk trade, which commanded enormous prices in Western markets.

As empires rose and collapsed—Roman, Byzantine, Mongol—the routes shifted and expanded, threading through Asia, the Middle East, and Europe. Buddhism spread west along these paths, Islam moved east, and Zoroastrianism found new footholds far from its Persian origins. Scientific and technical knowledge crossed the same ground.

The road's influence ran deeper than commerce. It shaped languages, artistic styles, and urban centers that grew wealthy precisely because they sat along its path. Its decline came gradually in the late medieval period, undercut by the opening of maritime trade routes and prolonged political instability across Central Asia. The overland networks became too dangerous and too slow to compete. Even so, the Silk Road left a lasting mark—proof that sustained contact between distant civilizations changes both of them in ways that outlast the trade itself.
