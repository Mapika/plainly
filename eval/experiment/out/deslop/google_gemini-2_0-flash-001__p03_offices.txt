## The Open-Plan Myth: Productivity or Pandemonium?

The open-plan office, touted as a hub of collaboration and innovation, rarely lives up to the promise. Proponents point to better communication and team cohesion, but for many workers the daily reality is noise, distraction, and no privacy.

Whatever benefits exist are drowned out by the constant hum of conversations, ringing phones, and general office clamor. Studies link open-plan environments to higher stress, poorer concentration, and lower productivity. The feeling of being perpetually watched discourages risk-taking and makes people reluctant to voice half-formed ideas.

Some people genuinely thrive in shared spaces. But a one-size-fits-all layout ignores the real differences in how people work. Introverts and anyone who needs sustained focus often end the day feeling wrung out rather than energized.

At its core, the open-plan office trades employee well-being for cheaper real estate. The fix isn't complicated: give people collaborative areas, quiet zones, and private spaces, then let them choose. Until companies commit to that, the open-plan office will remain a well-intentioned experiment that keeps failing the people it was meant to help.
