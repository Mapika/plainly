# A Beginner's Guide to Composting at Home

Composting turns kitchen scraps and yard waste into nutrient-rich soil for your garden while cutting down on landfill waste.

**Getting Started**

Choose a bin that fits your space. Outdoor options include tumblers or simple wooden bins. Apartment dwellers can use countertop containers or worm bins.

**What to Add**

Successful composting requires a balance of two ingredients:
- **Greens (nitrogen):** fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells, fresh grass clippings
- **Browns (carbon):** dry leaves, shredded cardboard, newspaper, straw

Aim for roughly 3 parts browns to 1 part greens.

**What to Avoid**

Skip meat, dairy, oily foods, and pet waste. These attract pests and create odors.

**Maintaining Your Pile**

Keep your compost as moist as a wrung-out sponge and turn it with a shovel or pitchfork every week or two. The added oxygen speeds decomposition and prevents bad smells.

**Harvesting**

In two to six months, your compost will look dark and crumbly, with an earthy smell. Mix it into garden beds, sprinkle it on your lawn, or use it for potted plants.

Start small, stay consistent, and you'll have usable compost before long.
