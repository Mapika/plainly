The Silk Road was a network of trade routes connecting Asia, Africa, and Europe, active from the 2nd century BCE to the mid-15th century CE. It began during China's Han Dynasty, initially moving silk, spices, tea, and porcelain westward in exchange for horses, wool, gold, and silver. But commerce was only part of the story. The routes also carried religions, technologies, and ideas across continents.

Buddhism traveled east along these paths and reshaped cultures throughout East Asia. Gunpowder, paper, and the compass moved westward from China, each leaving a mark on European history. Artistic styles and agricultural practices crossed borders the same way.

Cities that sat along the routes—Samarkand, Palmyra—grew into major centers of trade and scholarship, their fortunes tied directly to the flow of goods and people passing through. Empires expanded and consolidated power partly by controlling key stretches of the road.

Maritime trade eventually pulled commerce away from the overland routes, and the Silk Road's practical importance faded. Its influence, however, did not. The exchanges it enabled helped produce the modern world, and it remains a concrete example of how deeply interconnected human societies have always been.
