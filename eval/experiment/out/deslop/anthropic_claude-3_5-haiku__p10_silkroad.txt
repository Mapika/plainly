The Silk Road was an ancient network of trade routes connecting East Asia and the Mediterranean, operating roughly from 130 BCE to 1453 CE. Stretching over 4,000 miles across deserts, mountains, and grasslands, it enabled cultural and economic exchange on a scale previously unseen.

The network originated during China's Han Dynasty and took its name from the silk trade that drove much of its commerce. But merchants carried far more than silk — spices, precious metals, ceramics, and glass moved along these paths alongside pilgrims, monks, soldiers, and nomadic traders, producing a dense web of human interaction.

Rather than a single road, the Silk Road was a shifting system of interconnected routes linking China, India, Persia, Arabia, Greece, and Rome. Along these corridors, Buddhism and Islam spread into new territories, artistic styles crossed cultural boundaries, and technological knowledge passed between civilizations that would otherwise have had little contact.

Chinese, Persian, Arab, and European powers all drew wealth and ideas from the network. Cities positioned along its arteries — Samarkand, Chang'an, Baghdad — grew into cosmopolitan centers where scholars, merchants, and travelers mingled and exchanged knowledge.

Maritime trade routes expanded rapidly in the 15th century, gradually pulling commerce away from overland travel and diminishing the Silk Road's role. Even so, for over a millennium it had functioned as the primary mechanism through which Eurasian civilizations shaped one another — an early and consequential experiment in global connection.
