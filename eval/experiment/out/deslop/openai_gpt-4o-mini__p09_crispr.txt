CRISPR gene editing lets scientists modify an organism's DNA with a degree of precision that older techniques couldn't match. The name stands for "Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats," a natural defense system found in bacteria. Bacteria use it to store snippets of viral DNA, which they can recognize and target if the same virus attacks again.

The most widely used version, CRISPR-Cas9, works through two components: the Cas9 enzyme and a guide RNA. The guide RNA is designed to match a specific sequence in the target genome. Once inside the cell, it binds to that sequence and brings Cas9 to the exact spot where the cut needs to be made.

After Cas9 cuts the DNA, the cell's own repair machinery takes over. Scientists can steer that repair process to knock out a gene or insert new genetic material. The applications are broad: in medicine, researchers are pursuing treatments for inherited disorders; in agriculture, the technology has produced crops with improved pest resistance; in basic research, it has become a standard tool for probing what individual genes actually do. That combination of precision and flexibility is why CRISPR has become central to modern biotechnology.
