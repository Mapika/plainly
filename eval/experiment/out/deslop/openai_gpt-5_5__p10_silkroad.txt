The Silk Road was a vast network of trade routes connecting East Asia, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe for many centuries. It began taking shape during China's Han Dynasty around the 2nd century BCE, when Chinese envoys and merchants pushed westward and established new contacts. The name comes from Chinese silk, one of the most prized goods moving along the routes, but traders also carried spices, precious metals, glassware, horses, paper, and ceramics.

Commerce was only part of the story. The Silk Road became a major channel for religious and technological exchange. Buddhism spread from India into Central Asia and China along these routes, and Islam, Christianity, and other faiths traveled with merchant communities through the cities that dotted the way. Papermaking, printing, astronomy, and medical knowledge all crossed between civilizations along the same paths.

Trading hubs like Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar, and Xi'an grew wealthy and cosmopolitan as a result. The routes also connected powerful empires — the Han, Persian, Byzantine, and later the Mongol — and under Mongol rule in the 13th and 14th centuries, travel across Eurasia became safer and better organized than it had ever been.

Maritime trade eventually drew commerce away from the overland routes and diminished their role. Even so, the Silk Road stands as one of history's clearest examples of how distant societies shaped one another long before the modern era.
