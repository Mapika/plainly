# The Open-Plan Office Experiment Has Failed

Open-plan offices were sold as a revolution: tear down the walls, and collaboration, creativity, and camaraderie would flourish. Two decades on, the evidence points the other way.

A landmark Harvard study found that when companies switched to open layouts, face-to-face interaction dropped by around 70%, while email and messaging surged. Workers, overwhelmed by noise and constant visibility, retreated into headphones and digital silos. Rather than sparking spontaneous brainstorms, the open plan tends to produce a strange, performative quiet broken by distraction.

The costs are concrete. Open offices are linked to higher stress, more sick days, and lower productivity, particularly for work requiring sustained focus. Introverts suffer most, but no one thrives when every phone call becomes a public broadcast.

The appeal to employers is obvious: open plans are cheap, flexible, and project an image of modern egalitarianism. But cramming people together is not the same as bringing them together.

The better model offers quiet zones for focused work, comfortable spaces for genuine collaboration, and the option to retreat when needed. Offices should be designed around how people actually work, not around how managers wish they would.
