The rhythm of a long walk is a metronome for the mind. At first, thoughts clamor for attention, anxieties buzzing like trapped flies. The pace feels forced, the landscape a blur. But as the miles accumulate, something shifts. The buzzing fades, replaced by the steady thud of boots on pavement.

The world sharpens. Sunlight catches the dew on a spiderweb. I notice the intricate patterns in the bark of an old oak, a hawk circling overhead. Stress loosens its grip, layer by layer.

I learn that my body is stronger than I think, that discomfort passes, that the urge to turn back is usually just fear. One more step is enough to beat it. More than anything, I learn to stay present—to find something worth seeing in the ordinary, and to trust that the path will lead somewhere, even when I can't see where. The destination stops mattering as much as I thought it did.
