The Silk Road was a vast network of trade routes connecting East and West from the 2nd century BCE to the 15th century CE. Named after China's lucrative silk trade, it was never a single path but a web of land and sea routes stretching from the Korean Peninsula and Japan to the Mediterranean, passing through China, Central Asia, India, Persia, and the Arabian Peninsula.

Established during the Han Dynasty (202 BCE – 220 CE), the routes carried silk, spices, tea, and precious metals, but also moved ideas across continents. Buddhism traveled from India to East Asia. Christianity and Islam pushed into Central Asia and beyond. Technologies crossed borders too — papermaking, gunpowder, and the compass all moved along these corridors, reshaping the civilizations that received them.

Cultural exchange ran deep. Merchants and travelers brought their artistic traditions with them, and prolonged contact between distant cultures produced hybrid forms of art, literature, and architecture that neither civilization would have developed alone.

The Silk Road's decline came in the 15th century as maritime routes offered faster, more direct passages between Europe and Asia. Overland trade couldn't compete. Yet the network's influence persisted — in trade patterns, in the spread of religions and languages, in architectural forms that still stand across Central Asia. It remains the clearest historical example of how sustained contact between distant peoples reshapes all of them.
