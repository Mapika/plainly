Open-plan offices are widely promoted as the pinnacle of modern workspace design, yet they frequently disappoint. Proponents claim the layout encourages collaboration and idea-sharing, but the day-to-day reality is often noise, distraction, and lower productivity.

Without physical boundaries between workstations, sustained concentration becomes difficult. Constant background chatter creates stress and erodes motivation. There are also no private spaces for phone calls, sensitive conversations, or small meetings—tasks that make up a significant portion of most people's working day.

Open-plan offices also tend to favor appearance over function. The aesthetic appeal of an airy, unbroken floor plan comes at the expense of employees who need quiet to do their best work. For people with hearing impairments, the problem is compounded: an already noisy environment becomes genuinely hostile.

The case for open-plan offices rests on collaboration gains that research has largely failed to confirm. A 2018 Harvard study found that removing physical barriers actually reduced face-to-face interaction by around 70%, as employees turned to email and messaging to avoid the chaos around them. The design intended to bring people together pushed them apart.

It is time to move past the assumption that open layouts suit everyone. Workplaces should offer a genuine mix of spaces—quiet zones, enclosed rooms, and communal areas—so employees can choose the environment that fits the work in front of them. Good office design starts with people, not floor plans.
