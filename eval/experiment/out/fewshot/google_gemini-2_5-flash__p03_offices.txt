Open-plan offices, bless their cotton socks, are often touted as a modern marvel, a crucible for collaboration and a hotbed of spontaneous brilliance. In reality, they’re more like a particularly noisy, fluorescent-lit purgatory. You’re crammed cheek-by-jowl with Brenda from accounts, whose phone calls are less conversations and more public pronouncements, and Kevin from marketing, who hums tunelessly and cracks his knuckles with the regularity of a metronome.

The idea, I gather, is to foster a sense of community, but what typically blossoms is a festering resentment for anyone daring to breathe too loudly. Concentration becomes a mythical beast, chased away by the clatter of keyboards, the endless chitchat about last night’s telly, and the pungent aroma of someone’s reheated fish curry. If creativity is supposed to flourish in this environment, it's doing a remarkable job of hiding. Instead of innovative ideas, you get a symphony of distractions, and a workforce perpetually wearing headphones, not for music, but as a desperate plea for a sliver of peace. Give me a quiet corner, a door, and the blessed silence of my own thoughts any day.
