**A Beginner's Guide to Starting Your Own Compost Heap**

Let’s be honest: most of what we toss in the kitchen bin isn’t rubbish at all. It’s just forgotten food, waiting to become something better. Starting a compost pile is less about gardening science and more about giving your scraps a dignified retirement, where they can collapse into rich, dark earth.

You don’t need anything fancy. Find a spare corner of the garden, or get a simple bin. The magic happens by balancing two types of ingredients: your “greens” and your “browns.” Greens are the lively, nitrogen-rich stuff—vegetable peelings, coffee grounds, grass clippings. Browns are the dry, carbon-heavy matter—fallen leaves, shredded newspaper, egg cartons. Think of it as making a lasagna: a layer of greens, a layer of browns, and repeat. This keeps things from becoming a slimy, sour mess.

Avoid meat, dairy, and oils—they attract pests. Then, just let time and worms do the work. Turn the pile with a fork every few weeks if you’re feeling energetic; ignore it if you’re not. In a few months, you’ll find the bottom of the pile has transformed into a crumbly, sweet-smelling black gold. It’s a small, quiet rebellion against waste, and your plants will thank you for it.
