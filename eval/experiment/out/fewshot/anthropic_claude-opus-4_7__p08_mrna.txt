Here's the trick that makes mRNA vaccines so clever: they don't actually contain a virus, or even a piece of one. They contain instructions.

Think of mRNA as a recipe card. Your cells are constantly reading such cards — your own DNA sends them out by the thousands every second, telling the cellular machinery what proteins to build. An mRNA vaccine slips in a card of its own. The recipe, in the case of the COVID vaccines, is for the spike protein that studs the surface of the coronavirus.

Once injected, the mRNA is taken up by a few of your cells, which dutifully read the instructions and manufacture a batch of these spike proteins. The proteins themselves are harmless on their own — they're just a shape, a fragment, with no virus attached. But your immune system, ever paranoid, notices these unfamiliar shapes drifting around and mounts a response: antibodies, memory cells, the works. It learns the enemy's face before it ever meets the enemy.

The mRNA itself, having done its job, breaks down within days. What remains is the immunological memory — and the next time the real virus shows up, your body recognizes it instantly and goes to war.
