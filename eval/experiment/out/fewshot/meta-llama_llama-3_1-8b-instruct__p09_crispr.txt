Here's an explainer on CRISPR gene editing in a similarly human style:

CRISPR gene editing is like trying to cut a particularly knotty piece of twine. You need a pair of scissors, but not just any scissors - you need a pair that can snip through the thickest, most stubborn strands with precision and ease. That's basically what CRISPR does, but instead of scissors, it uses a molecular tool called an enzyme. This enzyme, called Cas9, is like a molecular ninja, slicing through the DNA double helix with surgical accuracy.

Here's how it works: first, you need to identify the specific spot in the DNA where you want to make the cut. It's like finding a specific page in a very long book. Then, you send in the Cas9 enzyme, which is like a molecular scout, to locate the target sequence and make the cut. The enzyme is guided by a piece of RNA, which is like a molecular map, that shows it exactly where to go.

Once the cut is made, the cell's natural repair machinery kicks in, and it tries to mend the break by stitching the DNA back together. But here's the clever part: you can provide a template for the cell to use, which is like giving it a blueprint for the repair. This allows you to insert new genetic material, essentially rewriting the cell's instructions. It's a powerful tool that's revolutionizing the field of genetics, and it's being used to treat everything from sickle cell anemia to cancer.
