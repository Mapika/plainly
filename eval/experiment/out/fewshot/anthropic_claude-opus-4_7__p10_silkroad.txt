The Silk Road was never really a road, and it was never really about silk—not entirely, anyway. It was a sprawling tangle of caravan routes that stitched together China, Central Asia, Persia, and the Mediterranean for the better part of two thousand years, beginning in earnest around the second century BC when the Han dynasty pushed westward and discovered there were people out there worth trading with.

Yes, silk moved along it—Roman senators' wives apparently developed quite the appetite for the stuff, much to the disgust of moralists who complained about gold draining east. But so did paper, gunpowder, horses, glass, spices, and slaves. More importantly, ideas traveled too. Buddhism walked into China on the back of a camel. Islam reached the steppes the same way. So did the bubonic plague, which in the fourteenth century rode the trade routes straight into Europe and killed off a third of it.

What makes the Silk Road matter isn't really the merchandise. It's that for centuries it was the closest thing humanity had to a worldwide web—a slow, dusty, dangerous one, certainly, but a network all the same. Civilizations that should have been strangers learned, instead, to borrow from one another.
