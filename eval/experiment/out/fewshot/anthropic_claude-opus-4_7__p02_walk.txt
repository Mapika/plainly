There is a particular kind of thinking that only happens at three miles per hour. Faster than that, the world blurs; slower, and you start fidgeting. But at a walking pace, with nowhere urgent to be, the mind unspools at the same rate as the road.

I have solved nothing on a long walk, and that is precisely the point. The problem I left the house with is usually still there when I get back, but it has been turned over a few times, like a stone in a creek, and some of the moss has come off. Sometimes I find I was asking the wrong question. More often I find I wasn't really asking a question at all, only complaining in the shape of one.

Walking teaches you that your legs know things your head refuses to admit. After an hour you cannot keep pretending you aren't tired, or sad, or in love with someone you shouldn't be. The body is an honest witness, and walking puts it on the stand.

And then there is the small, unfashionable lesson: that most distances are shorter than they look, and most hills are kinder once you are actually on them.
