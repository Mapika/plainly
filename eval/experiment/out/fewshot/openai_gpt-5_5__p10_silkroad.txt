The Silk Road was not a single road, but a great, shifting web of tracks, caravan routes, sea lanes, market towns, and mountain passes stretching from China across Central Asia to Persia, the Middle East, and Europe. It began to flourish around the second century BCE, when the Han dynasty opened contacts westward, and for many centuries it carried silk, spices, jade, horses, glass, paper, porcelain, and precious metals from one world to another.

But its real cargo was larger than merchandise. Along with bales and pack animals went religions, inventions, stories, diseases, and ideas. Buddhism traveled from India into China; Islam spread across Central Asia; paper-making, gunpowder, and printing moved westward; artistic styles and scientific knowledge crossed borders that armies could not permanently hold. Cities such as Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar, and Xi’an grew rich as meeting places of languages, beliefs, and ambitions.

The Silk Road also showed how dependent distant peoples could be upon one another long before the modern age of globalization. It made strangers profitable, and therefore sometimes tolerable. Though overland trade declined after sea routes became dominant, the Silk Road remains a powerful symbol of exchange: not merely of goods, but of civilization itself in motion.
