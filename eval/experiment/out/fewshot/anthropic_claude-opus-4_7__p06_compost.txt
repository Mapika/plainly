Composting at home is one of those things that seems complicated until you actually start doing it, and then you wonder why you waited so long. At its heart, composting is just letting nature do what it already wants to do—break stuff down—only in a corner of your yard or a bin on your balcony.

Begin with a container. A simple plastic bin with holes drilled in the sides will do, or a proper tumbler if you want to spend the money. Find a spot that gets a bit of sun but isn't baking all day.

The trick is balance. You need "greens" (vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings) and "browns" (dried leaves, cardboard, shredded newspaper). Aim for roughly three parts brown to one part green. Too much green and it'll smell like a swamp; too much brown and nothing happens at all.

Keep it about as damp as a wrung-out sponge, and turn it with a pitchfork or shovel every week or two to let air in. Skip meat, dairy, and oily foods—they attract pests and stink up the place.

In two or three months, you'll have dark, crumbly compost that smells like a forest floor. Your garden will thank you.
