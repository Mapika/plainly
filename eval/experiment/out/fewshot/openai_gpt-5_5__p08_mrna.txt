An mRNA vaccine is a set of instructions, not a miniature infection. It contains a short-lived molecule called messenger RNA, wrapped in a tiny fatty coat so it can slip into a few of our cells. The message tells those cells how to make one harmless piece of the virus—usually a surface protein, such as the spike protein of the coronavirus.

The cell reads the message much as a cook reads a recipe, makes the protein, and then destroys the mRNA. The protein, however, is noticed by the immune system. To the body it looks like a suspicious badge from an enemy uniform. Immune cells inspect it, learn its shape, and begin making antibodies and training T cells that can recognize it again.

If the real virus later appears, it is no longer a complete stranger. The immune system can respond faster and more strongly, often stopping infection early or reducing the severity of illness.

The vaccine does not enter the cell’s nucleus, where DNA is kept, and it does not rewrite a person’s genes. Its message is temporary; the training it provokes is the lasting part. In that sense, an mRNA vaccine is less like giving the body a disease than handing it a wanted poster before the outlaw rides into town.
