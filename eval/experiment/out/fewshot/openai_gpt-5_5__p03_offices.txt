Open-plan offices are an excellent idea if the object is to make everyone look busy while preventing them from doing any work. They have the air of democracy about them: no doors, no private dens, no executive fortresses. Everyone sits together in one bright, buzzing meadow of desks, as if ideas will float about like pollen and settle on the nearest laptop.

In practice, the pollen is mostly noise. One person is on a call, another is chewing something with architectural firmness, and a third is explaining last night’s television to someone who has plainly surrendered the will to live. The worker, meanwhile, is expected to produce careful thought in conditions better suited to a railway station.

It is said that open offices encourage collaboration. Sometimes they do. But collaboration is not the same thing as interruption, and conversation is not improved by being made compulsory. Good work needs both contact and solitude: the quick question, the planned meeting, and then the blessed right to shut a door, literal or otherwise.

So open-plan offices are not wholly bad, but they are badly oversold. They are cheaper to build than thoughtful workplaces, and that, I suspect, is the real secret of their popularity.
