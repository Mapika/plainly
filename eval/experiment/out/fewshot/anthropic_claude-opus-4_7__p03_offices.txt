The open-plan office was sold to us as a kind of liberation. Tear down the walls, the cubicles, the closed doors, and what springs up in their place will be collaboration, spontaneity, the happy collisions of minds. That was the pitch. The reality, as anyone who has actually worked in one can tell you, is a low and constant hum of other people's phone calls, the smell of someone's reheated fish, and a pair of headphones clamped permanently to your skull as a kind of acoustic fortress.

The strange thing is that we have studies on this now, and the studies say what common sense already suspected: people in open-plan offices talk to each other less, not more. They retreat into email and instant messages. They take more sick days. They get less done. The grand experiment in togetherness has produced, of all things, a deeper loneliness.

I suspect the truth is that the open plan was never really about collaboration at all. It was about square footage, and about managers being able to see, at a glance, who was at their desk. Call it what it is. But please, stop pretending it was designed with the worker in mind.
