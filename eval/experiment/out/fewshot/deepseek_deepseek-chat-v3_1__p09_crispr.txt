CRISPR gene editing is, at its heart, a biological copy-paste tool, borrowed from the simplest of creatures. In nature, bacteria use a system called CRISPR as a sort of immune memory. When a virus invades, the bacteria can snip a piece of the virus’s genetic code and file it away in a library within its own DNA. If that same virus returns, the bacteria can recognize it, locate it, and—using a molecular scalpel called Cas9—cut the invader’s genome to disable it.

Scientists have harnessed this simple, elegant mechanism. They can now design a short “guide” RNA, a genetic address label that leads the Cas9 enzyme to a precise, predetermined spot in the DNA of a plant, animal, or even human cell. Once there, Cas9 makes a clean cut in both strands of the DNA helix.

This cut is the crucial moment. The cell’s own repair machinery rushes to fix the break. Scientists can exploit this natural process. They can introduce a new, corrected snippet of DNA for the cell to use as a patch, effectively “editing” the gene by inserting a desired sequence. Alternatively, with a less precise repair, they can simply disable a faulty gene. It’s a way of rewriting the fundamental code of life with a precision that was unimaginable just a generation ago.
