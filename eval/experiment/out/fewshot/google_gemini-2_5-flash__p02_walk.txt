A long walk, now there's a thing. You set out, boots laced, maybe a faint hum of music in your ears, and you think you know where you're going. But that's the first lesson: you don't. Not really. The path shifts, the light changes, and suddenly that familiar corner looks entirely new. Your mind, usually a buzzing hive of chores and worries, slowly starts to quiet. The frantic to-do lists recede, replaced by the rhythm of your own breath, the crunch of gravel underfoot.

You notice things you'd never see from a car window – the intricate pattern of a fallen leaf, the determined push of a weed through cracked pavement, a distant bird's call echoing just so. And then, without even realizing it, you start to untangle the knots in your own head. Problems that seemed insurmountable back at the desk begin to soften, to reframe themselves. The truth is, a long walk is less about going somewhere and more about coming home to yourself. It's a gentle reminder that sometimes, the best way to see clearly is to put one foot in front of the other, and just walk.
