Composting is simply the art of letting yesterday’s scraps become tomorrow’s soil. You do not need a farm, a beard, or a mysterious steaming heap at the edge of a field. A small bin, a little patience, and a willingness to save your vegetable peelings will do.

Begin with a container. If you have a yard, use a compost bin or a loose pile in a shady corner. If you live in an apartment, try a sealed countertop pail for collecting scraps and a worm bin or municipal compost service.

Feed your compost a mixture of “greens” and “browns.” Greens are moist, lively things: fruit peels, vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, tea leaves, and fresh grass clippings. Browns are dry, papery things: fallen leaves, shredded newspaper, cardboard, straw, and dry twigs. Too many greens make a sour, soggy mess; too many browns slow everything down. Aim for roughly two or three parts browns to one part greens.

Do not add meat, dairy, oily foods, or pet waste. They invite smells, pests, and regret.

Turn the pile now and then with a fork or shovel, and keep it damp like a wrung-out sponge. In a few months, the scraps will darken into crumbly compost that smells like a forest floor. Mix it into garden beds or pots, and begin again.
