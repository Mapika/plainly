People should read more books because a book is one of the few remaining places where the mind is not being hurried along like a sheep in a chute. Most of our daily information arrives chopped into little shining pieces, each one shouting to be noticed and forgotten. A book does something different. It asks us to sit still, to follow a thought past the first bend in the road, to live for a while under another person’s weather.

Reading makes a person larger on the inside. You borrow courage from people who have endured worse than you. You borrow wit from people who saw clearly through the fog. You meet fools, saints, cowards, tyrants, lovers, failures, and occasionally someone uncomfortably like yourself. This is useful. A person who has met many kinds of people in books is less likely to be shocked by the variety of people in life.

Books also give language back to us. They put names on feelings we had only carried around as vague aches. They sharpen the private instrument by which we think. To read well is not to escape the world, but to return to it better equipped: less lonely, less easily fooled, and perhaps a little more awake.
